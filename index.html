GSFC Citizen Science Task Group

Overview

We are a community of citizen science project managers using crowdsourcing to advance science education and scientific discovery. Citizen science projects at GSFC span the fields of astrophysics, earth science, heliophysics, and planetary science.

Get Involved

Astrophysics

Andromeda Project (Zooniverse)

A multi-treasury program collecting data at Hubble Space Telscope resolution across 40% of the Andromeda Galaxy (M31) was examined by volunteers to identify star clusters for further analysis. Many follow up projects have been accomplished providing insight into star cluster formation and the range of cluster ages and environments in M31.

Website: https://www.andromedaproject.org/
Contact: Dr. Carol Christian, carolc@stsci.edu

Backyard Worlds: Planet 9 (Zooniverse)

Is there a large planet at the fringes of our solar system awaiting discovery, a world astronomers call Planet Nine? We’re looking for this planet and for new brown dwarfs in the backyard of the solar system using data from NASA's Wide-field Infrared Survey Explorer (WISE) mission. But we need your help! Finding these dim objects requires combing through the images by eye to distinguish moving celestial bodies from ghosts and other artifacts. There are too many images for us to search through by ourselves. So come join the search, and you might find a rogue world that's nearer to the Sun than Proxima Centauri---or even the elusive Planet Nine. Launched Feb 15, 2017.

Website: http://www.backyardworlds.org/
Contact: Dr. Marc Kuchner, marc.j.kuchner@nasa.gov

Disk Detective (Zooniverse)

Search for protoplanetary disks in WISE data - WISE is a NASA mission surveying the whole sky in infrared. This project is looking at stars to find dusty debris disks, similar to our asteroid field. These disks suggest that these stars are in the early stages of forming planetary systems. Learning more about these stars can tell us how our Solar System formed. Computers often confuse debris disks around stars with other astronomical objects. Disk Detective needs your help to sort out what stars actually have these disks from galaxies and nebulae. Launched February, 2014.

Website: http://www.diskdetective.org/
Contact: Dr. Marc Kuchner, marc.j.kuchner@nasa.gov

Galaxy Zoo: Hubble (Zooniverse)

The data included material from several multi-treasury Hubble Space Telescope programs. The purpose was to understand how the morphologies of galaxies change with a number of factors such as redshift, environment, black hole formation and clustering. Launched July 2007.

Website: https://www.galaxyzoo.org/
Contact: Dr. Carol Christian, carolc@stsci.edu

Planet Hunters (Zooniverse)

Look for eclipses made by extrasolar planets in photometry from NASA’s Kepler mission. The data, displayed 28-day chunks, shows how the brightness of a star changes over time. This project is run out of Yale, but has a strong NASA tie.

Website: https://www.planethunters.org/
Contact: Brian Dunbar, brian.dunbar@nasa.gov

Star Date: M83 (Zooniverse)

The data campaign of Hubble Space Telescope observations on the Antennae galaxies resulted in a catalog of star clusters. The volunteers in Star Date classified star clusters according to appearance and color resulting in a rough age assignment for each object. The complication in providing ages for clusters is that these objects are often either embedded in nebulae or superimposed on background complex structure, making automated processes challenging.

Website: http://www.projectstardate.org/
Contact: Dr. Carol Christian, carolc@stsci.edu

Data Mining Twitter for Augmenting NASA Precipitation Research and Applications (GSFC)

Project funded by the Citizen Science for Earth Systems Program, NASA ROSES NNH16ZDA001N-CSESP): This project explores the feasibility of extracting from the Twitter data stream useful information for application to NASA precipitation research, in particular, augmenting the existing validation program of the Global Precipitation Measurement (GPM) Mission. We are engaging with both ‘passive’ and ‘active’ participants to contribute to the project. Our general crowd-sourcing strategy is to not require participants to sign-up or install some app to contribute as citizen scientists. This is a more robust approach to gaining a large source of crowd. Our framework for processing Twitter data is currently focused on classifying tweets, determining quality, developing a data model, and co-locating tweets with satellite data. This framework will be generic, i.e., not specific to a given measurement, social medium, or satellite mission.

Website: http://commons.esipfed.org/node/9627
Contact: Bill Teng, william.l.teng@nasa.gov

GLOBE Observer

The Global Learning and Observations to Benefit the Environment (GLOBE) Program is an international science and education program that provides students and the public worldwide with the opportunity to participate in data collection and the scientific process, and contribute meaningfully to our understanding of the Earth system and global environment. Announced by the U.S. Government on Earth Day in 1994, GLOBE launched its worldwide implementation in 1995. The Citizen Science arm of GLOBE is the GLOBE Observer Program. . The GLOBE Observer Program currently accepts cloud observations with a planned expansion. The cloud observations help NASA scientists understand clouds from below (ground level) and above (from space). Since GLOBE Observer is part of the GLOBE program, citizen scientists working on GLOBE Observer are also providing data for student research, strengthening science education. Other applications currently being developed for GLOBE Observer are Mosquito Habitat Mapper and Adopt-a-Pixel (for Land Cover, Land Use).

Website: https://observer.globe.gov/en
Contact: Holli Riebeek Kohl, Kristen Weaver

Image Detective

This project harnesses the power of the people to analyze astronaut photos and add the needed metadata that allows these photos to be used in science. Astronauts aboard the International Space Station have taken more than 1.5 million photos that aren’t labeled with location or content information. Using image detectives, participants use a series of checkboxes to tag the images as various geologic and manmade features, try to locate the image center point on a Google Map, and have the option to type in anything additional they would like to say in the image (for instance, transient features like forest fires or flooding).

Website: https://cosmoquest.org/x/beta/
Contact: Dr. Pamela Gay, science@cosmoquest.org

Landslide Reporter

Landslide Reporter is an online web application where citizen scientists can submit, view, and analyze information about landslides worldwide from their web browser or mobile device. Reports made with Landslide Reporter detailing when, where, and how a landslide occurred are added to the Global Landslide Catalog (GLC), the largest openly available global database of all rainfall-triggered mass movements since 2007. In a separate application, Landslide Viewer, citizen scientists can view all approved landslide reports on the world map or download the GLC. The GLC currently contains more than 11,000 landslide reports from media sources like news articles and other databases, but it is hard to maintain. Thus, citizen science enables improvement in quality and quantity of the database, resulting in a more robust picture of all landslides around the world. Project launching in early 2018.

Website: /citizen-science/landslides
Contact: Caroline Juang, caroline.juang@nasa.gov, Dr. Dalia Kirschbaum, Thomas Stanley

Heliophysics

Aurorasaurus (NSF/NASA)

This project gathers real-time data about aurora sightings and sends out notifications to users when the Northern Lights are likely visible in their area. Aurorasaurus will significantly improve forecasting of the aurora using citizen science reports and crowd-sourced (Twitter) ground truth observations of aurora. Registered users get location-based notifications, a real-time monitor of space weather activity, the capability to help verify tweets and search for real sightings, answers to science and aurora questions, and more. Aurorasaurus was built by scientists for the public. The project was funded by an award from the National Science Foundation.

Website: http://www.aurorasaurus.org/
Contact: Dr. Elizabeth MacDonald, elizabeth.a.macdonald@nasa.gov

Planetary Science

Be a Martian (NASA)

Using a sophisticated Mars visualizer, you will help NASA scientists piece together from hundreds of thousands of separate images, a mammoth mosaic image of the surface of Mars. You can also help NASA geographically tag all of the Martian craters to create a complete catalog.

Website: http://beamartian.jpl.nasa.gov/welcome
Contact: Michelle Viotti, michelle.a.viotti@jpl.nasa.gov

Lunar Impact Monitoring (NASA)

Look for flashes on the dark portion of the moon to help NASA count new impacts - Use Earth-based observations of the dark portion of the moon to establish the rates and sizes of large meteoroids (greater than 10s of grams or a few ounces in mass) striking the lunar surface. Why it is important? The Meteoroid Environment Office is charged with developing an understanding of the meteoroid environment. Lunar impact monitoring enables measurement of meteoroids in the 10s of grams to kilograms size range which are difficult to measure with other techniques.

Website: https://www.nasa.gov/centers/marshall/news/lunar/index.html
Contact: Danielle Moser, danielle.e.moser@nasa.gov

Mappers (CosmoQuest)

This suite of projects engages the public in helping map out rocky worlds. Science teams can request that citizen scientists mark crater edges, trace out boulder fields, measure the radii of large boulders or other geologic features, and map crater ejecta. These projects regular change and new data is constantly added to support new science. Current studies serve both scientific studies and missions that need to perform site selection and create hazard maps. Current projects include Mars Mapper, Mercury Mapper, Moon Mapper, and Vesta Mapper.

Website: http://cosmoquest.org
Contact: science@cosmoquest.org

Planet Four: Ridges (Zooniverse)

This project invites volunteers to search for polygonal ridge features in Mars Reconnaissance Orbiter imagery! This project came out of a collaboration among participants in a September Our Red Planet workshop on public participation in the Mars exploration program.

Website: https://www.zooniverse.org/projects/mschwamb/planet-four-ridges
Contact: Dr. Laura Kerber, laura.a.kerber@jpl.nasa.gov
